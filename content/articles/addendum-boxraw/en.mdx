# What a Boxing Brand Can Teach Wealth Advisors, Software Studios, and Architects About Building a Brand

## Applying the BOXRAW model to three expert practices that have everything — except a brand system that shows it

_By Mari Miavka · Reading time: 12 minutes_
_Addendum 1 to: [BOXRAW Made $8 Million in Two Years by Not Selling Clothes](/articles/boxraw-eight-million)_

---

## Introduction

_Reading time: 1 minute_

If you read the main article, you already have the framework. Six philosophical levers. Seven sales mechanics. Eight lifecycle elements. All demonstrated through BOXRAW — a boxing apparel brand that built a $8M cult without a single paid influencer.

But maybe you were reading and thinking: _"This is fascinating. But I don't own a gym or a sportswear brand. I manage wealth. I build software. I design buildings. How does any of this apply to me?"_

This addendum is for you.

I chose three professions that look nothing like sport — but operate by the exact same brand logic. What connects them: they are all **founder-led practices where the person is the brand**. And in all three, the same pattern keeps repeating. Years of real expertise. Real results for real clients. And online — nothing. A generic LinkedIn profile. A website that looks like 50 others. A reputation that lives entirely in private referrals and dies there.

BOXRAW didn't start with a massive budget. They started with one clear idea about who they were and who they were for. Then they built a system around that idea.

That's exactly what's missing in most expert practices. Not the expertise. The system that makes the expertise visible.

Here's how it works for each of the three.

---

## Part 1. The Wealth Advisor

_Reading time: 4 minutes_

> _Switzerland is the world's largest offshore wealth management center. Thousands of independent advisors. Almost none of them have a brand — only a service._

### The gap no one talks about

The Swiss wealth management industry was built on relationships and confidentiality. Referrals came through private networks. Marketing was considered unnecessary — even slightly vulgar. Real clients found real advisors through other real clients.

That model worked for twenty years. It's breaking now.

The new generation of wealth management clients — founders who just sold their companies, entrepreneurs in their first decade of serious wealth, expat families navigating multi-jurisdiction complexity — **research online before the first meeting**. They read. They look at LinkedIn. They search for opinions and thinking. And if there's nothing there — they move to the next name on the list.

This is a massive gap for the advisor who understands it first.

Most independent wealth advisors have the same online presence: a professional headshot, a list of services that sounds identical to every competitor, and a vague statement about "comprehensive wealth management for discerning clients." Nothing that tells you who this person actually is, what they believe, or why you'd choose them over the twenty others with the same credentials.

**The gap: world-class expertise, invisible brand.**

### The BOXRAW model applied

**Lever 1: One idea that cuts**

BOXRAW built everything on four words: _Boxing is Way of Life._ Not "we sell boxing apparel." One phrase that answered every question about what they stood for and who they were for.

Most wealth advisors describe themselves as "wealth management for high-net-worth individuals." This tells you nothing. It applies to everyone. It belongs to no one.

What would it look like to have one idea that cuts?

- _"Wealth advisory for Swiss-based founders in their first decade of serious capital"_ — this tells the client, the referral source, and the market exactly who you serve and when
- _"Tax-first wealth planning for entrepreneurs building cross-border"_ — this signals a specific methodology before the first meeting
- _"Wealth architecture for families navigating the transition from earned income to invested capital"_ — this names the exact emotional and financial moment your ideal client is in

One idea. Not three service pillars. One phrase that makes the right client think: _"That's exactly me."_

**Lever 2: Internal credibility**

Ben Amanna spoke the language of someone who had been boxing since age twelve. The details he used were only legible to insiders. Outsiders felt the authenticity without understanding the specifics. Insiders recognized one of their own.

The wealth advisor equivalent: the details only serious clients recognize.

Not "ROI optimization" or "diversified portfolio strategy." Those phrases belong to everyone. Instead: the specific mechanics of unwinding a concentrated equity position after a liquidity event. The real tradeoffs in a Swiss holding structure for a tech founder with US options. What actually happens to a family's decision-making when liquid wealth suddenly exceeds earned income.

These details look "too specific" from the outside. To the right client, they sound like: _this advisor has seen this before. This advisor knows my situation._

**Lever 3: Emotion over function**

BOXRAW doesn't sell training apparel. They sell _Boxing is Love_ — a return to the body, clarity of mind, the discipline of showing up daily as a form of self-respect.

What does a wealth advisor actually sell?

Not "portfolio management." Not "tax efficiency." The client isn't buying a service — they're buying **freedom from financial anxiety in the most consequential decade of their life**. The ability to say yes to an opportunity without running the numbers at 2am. The confidence to make a decision about their children's future without feeling like they might be wrong.

That's the emotion. Name it. Build your communication around it, not around the technical features of your service.

**Lever 4: Lifecycle architecture — the ongoing relationship**

BOXRAW built a tiered loyalty system where client engagement deepened over time and rewards compounded. The client who stayed longest got the most.

For a wealth advisor, the lifecycle equivalent is the annual review — but elevated. Not a compliance call. A **designed touchpoint** that signals: I remember where you were last year. I know what changed. Here is what I think it means for the next three years.

Add to that: a quarterly note with one idea relevant to your specific client's situation (not a mass newsletter). A birthday message that mentions something personal. A first meeting ritual that every new client goes through and that signals: this is how we work here.

These are the rituals that turn clients into advocates. Not only performance — the experience of being known.

### What to do this month

Write your one idea that cuts. Not a mission statement. One sentence. The more specific about who you serve and when, the better. Test it in your next five conversations with potential clients. Watch for the reaction: _"That's exactly me"_ means you found it.

## Part 2. The Custom Software Studio

_Reading time: 4 minutes_

> _72% of Swiss companies can't find the IT talent they need. The market is flooded with anonymous agencies that look identical. The studio that understands brand wins._

### The gap no one talks about

Look at ten custom software agencies in Switzerland. Their websites are nearly interchangeable. "Innovation. Quality. Partnership." Stock photos of people looking at screens. Lists of technologies. Mission statements that could belong to any company in any country.

This is not a talent problem or a quality problem. It's a brand problem.

Clients making six-figure decisions about software partners are trying to answer one question: _Who can I trust with something this important?_ Technical credentials answer part of that question. Trust comes from knowing who someone is, what they believe, and what they'll do when things get hard.

**The gap: technical capability without cultural identity.**

### The BOXRAW model applied

**Lever 1: Deliberately breaking the category pattern**

BOXRAW entered a market where every brand looked like fight gear. They went the opposite direction: streetwear aesthetic, clean typography, and a language closer to poetry than warfare.

Most software studios look like technology corporations: cold, anonymous, interchangeable.

What does the opposite look like? A strong point of view, founder-led writing, and visual/editorial identity that feels human and memorable.

**Lever 2: The Refusal List**

BOXRAW made a deliberate choice about who they were for — and who they were not for.

The software studio equivalent: a public **Refusal List**.

> _We don't build for crypto. We don't take projects under a threshold. We don't work with founders who can't define success. We don't promise timelines we haven't stress-tested._

When you name who you don't want, the people you do want feel chosen.

**Lever 3: The founder as the voice**

BOXRAW holds together around one person. The thinking is personal, not corporate.

A software studio can do the same. Founder-led essays, real opinions, and practical breakdowns of decisions clients rarely see.

In an industry of anonymous agencies, a real voice wins attention and trust.

**Lever 4: Seeding over advertising**

BOXRAW seeded product inside real boxing culture before spending on loud promotion.

For a software studio: seed expertise into the communities that matter. Publish meaningful postmortems. Offer architecture reviews. Share what works and what failed.

This is the software equivalent of cultural seeding.

### What to do this month

Write your Refusal List. Make it specific. Publish it. Measure the quality of inquiries after 30 days.

## Part 3. The Independent Architect

_Reading time: 3 minutes_

> _Architects understand identity through space. Most never apply that same intelligence to their own brand._

### The gap no one talks about

Switzerland has a strong market of independent architecture studios, especially in Zurich, Basel, Lausanne, and Geneva. Competition is intense and clients are discerning.

The paradox: architects create brand experiences through space for clients, then present their own practice online with an anonymous portfolio and no voice.

**The gap: spatial intelligence without brand identity.**

### The BOXRAW model applied

**Lever 1: Concreteness over abstraction**

Most architecture sites say: _"We create beautiful spaces."_ This belongs to everyone.

BOXRAW used concrete, ownable language.

The architecture equivalent might be:

- _"Concrete, oak, and daylight. Nothing else."_
- _"We refuse projects that prioritize photography over inhabitation."_
- _"One local craftsman in every core build."_

These are operating principles, not slogans.

**Lever 2: Story, not biography**

BOXRAW's origin is memorable because it has a turning point and emotion.

Most architecture bios read like CVs. A story answers a better question: _Why does this studio exist in the first place?_

That answer is what makes people remember and trust you.

**Lever 3: Internal credibility**

The details only insiders notice build trust with serious clients.

For architects: technical material choices, construction-stage decisions, and process rituals that prove you know what you are doing beyond pretty renders.

**Lever 4: Annual ritual**

BOXRAW built rhythms people return to.

An architect can do the same through annual open studios, yearly reflection publications, or curated client salons. Rituals turn an audience into a community.

### What to do this month

Rewrite your About page as a story, not a biography. Find the turning point. Two paragraphs. Test it on someone outside your field.
